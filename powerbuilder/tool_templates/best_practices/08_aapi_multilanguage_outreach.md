# AAPI Multi-Language Outreach: Translation, Framing, and Channel Notes

**Source:** Powerbuilder curated corpus, distilled from APIAVote field reports, AAPI Data civic engagement surveys, Pew Research AAPI political surveys, and Analyst Institute in-language messaging studies.
**Date:** 2025-10-04
**Document type:** field playbook + translation guidance
**Topics:** aapi, multi-language, chinese, vietnamese, korean, tagalog, hindi, translation, in-language, civic engagement

## Why in-language outreach matters

The AAPI electorate is the fastest-growing in the US (around 15 million eligible voters in 2024) and the most linguistically diverse. Roughly **one in three AAPI eligible voters speaks a language other than English at home**, and **one in four prefers to receive civic and political information in a language other than English** (AAPI Data 2024 survey). English-only outreach systematically underperforms with this electorate, and the underperformance is not uniform: it concentrates in the foreign-born adult-naturalized segment, which is also the segment with the highest persuadability on civic-engagement asks.

For a c3 voter education program, in-language is not a nice-to-have. It is the difference between reaching the household decision-maker and being filtered out by the bilingual adult child who answers the door.

## The five languages this file covers

These are the highest-leverage languages for US AAPI civic outreach by combined population, language preference, and registration gap:

1. **Chinese** — Simplified (Mainland-origin) and Traditional (Taiwan-origin, Hong Kong-origin). Treat as two scripts, not one.
2. **Vietnamese**
3. **Korean**
4. **Tagalog** (Filipino)
5. **Hindi**

Other meaningful languages (Japanese, Thai, Khmer, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Gujarati) are not covered here and require their own treatments. Do not auto-translate from these five into a sixth without native-speaker review.

## Translation methodology that works

- **Translate from the strategy, not from the English copy.** Brief a bilingual writer on the audience, the ask, and the call to action. Let them write the in-language version directly. A literal translation of the English door script reads stilted and trips the "this is not for me" reflex.
- **Pair with a back-translation review.** A second bilingual reader translates the in-language version back to English. If the back-translation drifts from the original strategy, the in-language copy is doing something the strategist did not intend.
- **Use community readers, not commercial translation services, for civic messaging.** Commercial vendors translate accurately and tonally wrong. The voice of a 1.5-generation Korean-American organizer reaches Korean households better than the voice of a Seoul-trained translator.
- **Avoid transliteration of English political terms.** "Voter registration" rendered phonetically into Korean or Vietnamese reads as a non-translation. Use the in-language civic vocabulary, even if it is less familiar to second-generation readers.
- **Test creative with focus groups segmented by generation and language dominance.** The 65-year-old Vietnamese-dominant voter and the 30-year-old English-dominant Vietnamese-American voter are not in the same audience.

## Chinese (Simplified and Traditional)

**Population context:** ~5.5M Chinese Americans, concentrated in CA, NY, NJ, MA, WA, TX, GA. Roughly 60 percent foreign-born among adults. Mandarin is the dominant spoken language; Cantonese remains significant in older Bay Area, NYC Chinatown, and Boston communities.

**Script choice:**
- Simplified for Mainland-origin households (post-1980 immigration cohort, common in Sun Belt and newer suburbs).
- Traditional for Taiwan-origin and Hong Kong-origin households (often older immigration cohort, common in established Chinese-American neighborhoods in the Northeast and Bay Area).
- When in doubt in a mixed-origin DMA: produce both. The cost difference is marginal once the translation exists.

**Voice and framing:**
- Civic-engagement framing lands better than rights-framing. "Your vote shapes your community's schools and small businesses" outperforms "exercise your right to vote."
- Family and intergenerational framing is strong: "voting is something we do for our children and our parents."
- Avoid framings that sound like a request for personal information. The 1990s-2000s wave of immigration-related anxieties is still present.

**Sample door opener (Mandarin, Simplified, romanized for the playbook):** "您好，我是社区志愿者，今天我们在这一带跟邻居们聊聊关于学校和社区服务的问题。" ("Hello, I am a community volunteer, today we are in this neighborhood talking with residents about schools and community services.")

**Channel notes:** WeChat is the dominant in-language information channel for Mainland-origin households. Paid placement on WeChat is restricted; partner with established in-language community accounts. WhatsApp is significant for Hong Kong-origin and some Taiwan-origin households. Spotify and YouTube reach the under-40 segment.

## Vietnamese

**Population context:** ~2.2M Vietnamese Americans, heavily concentrated in CA (Orange County, San Jose), TX (Houston), WA, GA (Gwinnett, Clayton), VA (Northern VA). Among the most language-dominant of the major AAPI groups: roughly 40 percent of adults prefer information in Vietnamese.

**Voice and framing:**
- Refugee-origin community memory is central. Civic engagement is framed as participation in the country that received them; this framing is bipartisan and works for nonpartisan voter education without invoking partisan refugee-policy debates.
- Family decision-making is strong; the head-of-household frame matters more than in many other AAPI communities.
- Catholic and Buddhist religious community channels are both meaningful; outreach via temple or parish bulletin partnerships outperforms generic civic mail.

**Sample text-message opener (Vietnamese):** "Chào anh/chị, [tên của bạn] từ [tên tổ chức]. Cuộc bầu cử địa phương sẽ diễn ra vào [ngày]. Để biết địa điểm bỏ phiếu của anh/chị, xem tại [link]." ("Hello, [your name] from [organization name]. The local election will be held on [date]. To find your polling place, see [link].")

**Channel notes:** Vietnamese-language radio (Little Saigon Radio in OC, Saigon Network in San Jose, Saigon Houston Radio) reaches the over-50 audience reliably. Facebook is dominant for the 30 to 60 segment. TikTok skews under 30. SBTN (Saigon Broadcasting Television Network) reaches a national diasporic audience but skews older and more politically engaged.

## Korean

**Population context:** ~1.9M Korean Americans, concentrated in CA (LA, Orange County), NY/NJ, GA (Gwinnett, Duluth), VA, TX. Roughly 70 percent foreign-born among adults; high educational attainment but persistent civic-engagement gap relative to other AAPI groups.

**Voice and framing:**
- Korean-American voters respond to community-collective framing ("우리 동네," "our neighborhood") more than individual-rights framing.
- The 1.5-generation (immigrated as children) is a critical bridge audience and disproportionately served as messengers.
- Religious community is significant: Korean-American Christian church networks are central civic information channels in most metros.

**Sample phone opener (Korean, romanized for playbook):** "안녕하세요, 저는 [이름]이고 [단체 이름]에서 전화드립니다. 다가오는 [날짜] 선거에 대해 잠깐 이야기 나누고 싶어서 연락드렸습니다." ("Hello, this is [name] calling from [organization name]. I wanted to briefly talk with you about the upcoming election on [date].")

**Channel notes:** KakaoTalk is the dominant in-language messaging channel; paid placement is limited but organic community channels are powerful. Korean-language daily papers (Korea Daily, Korea Times) still reach the over-50 audience. YouTube Korean-language civic channels are growing fast. Naver reaches Mainland-Korea-connected households but is a smaller US audience.

## Tagalog (Filipino)

**Population context:** ~4.4M Filipino Americans, the second-largest Asian-origin group in the US, concentrated in CA (Daly City, San Diego, LA County), HI, NV, NJ, IL, TX. **Important nuance:** English is widely spoken, and many Filipino Americans prefer English for civic information. Tagalog outreach lands best with the over-55, foreign-born, religiously-active segment.

**Voice and framing:**
- Bayanihan (community helping community) is a real and effective frame, not a cliché.
- Catholic parish networks are central; civic-engagement collateral distributed through parish bulletins reaches a meaningful share of older Filipino voters.
- Healthcare-worker community (a significant share of Filipino Americans) is a strong messenger channel.

**Sample door opener (Taglish, which is how most Filipino Americans actually speak):** "Hi po, I am [name] from [organization]. We are talking to neighbors about the upcoming election. Para sa schools and community natin." (Mixed Tagalog and English, "po" is the respectful particle, "Para sa schools and community natin" means "for our schools and community.")

**Channel notes:** Facebook is dominant across all age segments; Filipino American Facebook groups by hometown or province of origin are strong organic channels. TFC (The Filipino Channel) reaches the over-50 audience. YouTube and TikTok reach the under-40 segment. SMS is well-received.

## Hindi

**Population context:** ~900K Hindi-speaking Indian Americans (the broader South Asian American electorate is ~5.4M and includes Punjabi, Gujarati, Bengali, Urdu, Tamil, Telugu, and Malayalam speakers; Hindi alone covers a meaningful but not dominant share). Concentrated in NJ, NY, CA (Bay Area, LA), TX (Houston, Dallas), GA (Gwinnett, Forsyth), IL.

**Caveat:** Hindi-language outreach lands well with the foreign-born adult-naturalized segment but **misses the second-generation entirely** (who are English-dominant) and **alienates non-Hindi South Asians** (Tamil, Telugu, Bengali, Punjabi households read Hindi-only outreach as exclusionary). Use Hindi for targeted households and English for the general South Asian universe.

**Voice and framing:**
- Family and education framing is consistently strong.
- Community-temple, gurdwara, and mosque networks are channel-specific to the religious community in the household; do not collapse them.
- Avoid Hindi political terms borrowed from Indian-political-context vocabulary; they carry partisan connotations from the Indian electoral context that do not map to US civic engagement.

**Sample mailer headline (Hindi, Devanagari):** "आपकी आवाज़, आपके समुदाय के लिए" ("Your voice, for your community.")

**Channel notes:** WhatsApp is the dominant in-language channel; family and friend group chats are how civic information actually moves. TV Asia and Zee TV reach the over-45 audience. YouTube reaches all segments. South Asian community radio in NJ, Houston, and Bay Area is meaningful.

## Cross-language operational notes

- **Universe sizing:** voter files do not reliably code AAPI subgroups beyond a generic "Asian" flag. Use surname-based modeling (cautiously, with documented error rates) plus self-reported language preference where partners have collected it. Do not assume the file is right.
- **Polling-location callouts:** in every language, name the polling place by location ("the elementary school on Pleasant Hill Road"), not just the address. AAPI voters in newer suburban developments have low confidence in the assigned precinct.
- **Translated literature:** print double-sided English on one side, in-language on the other. Single-language pieces look like the campaign is excluding the rest of the household.
- **Cadence:** the mail and door cadence used for Latinx GOTV (see file 01) translates well to Vietnamese and Filipino communities. Korean and Chinese communities respond better to a lighter mail cadence and a heavier in-language digital cadence.

## What does not work

- Auto-translation tools (Google Translate, DeepL) for the final creative. Use them only for first drafts that a community reader will rewrite.
- Hiring a single "AAPI translator" to cover multiple languages. Each language needs its own native writer.
- Treating "AAPI voters" as a single audience. The 2024 cycle research consistently shows the variance within AAPI groups is larger than the variance between AAPI and non-AAPI averages.
- Stock photography of a generic "Asian family" on collateral. Use community-sourced or commissioned imagery; otherwise the audience reads the campaign as not-for-them.
