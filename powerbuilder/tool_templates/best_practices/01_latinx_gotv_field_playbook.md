# Latinx GOTV Field Playbook (Southeast US)

**Source:** Powerbuilder curated corpus, distilled from Analyst Institute, Voto Latino, and Mi Familia Vota field reports.
**Date:** 2025-09-15
**Document type:** field playbook
**Topics:** latinx, gotv, canvassing, spanish, southeast, georgia

## Why bilingual matters in Gwinnett and similar Sun Belt suburbs

The Latinx electorate in Gwinnett County, GA is **majority US-born and bilingual**, with a sizable foreign-born minority (~40% of adults) for whom Spanish is the primary household language. A door knock that defaults to English will lose the trust of the Spanish-dominant voter in the home and miss the family conversation that drives turnout. A door knock that defaults to Spanish in an English-dominant household reads as a non-ask. The right move is **lead in Spanish, switch on cue**: open with one Spanish sentence, then ask "¿Prefiere que sigamos en español o en inglés?"

## Door-knock structure that converts

1. Smile, give your first name only, and the campaign name. No clipboard in hand at the door — keep it tucked under your arm.
2. One Spanish opener. If the voter answers in English, switch immediately and warmly.
3. State the *reason for the visit* in one sentence — never "do you have a minute," always "estoy por aquí esta semana hablando con vecinos sobre [issue]."
4. Ask one open question tied to a *local* issue (school, road, hospital, water bill). National issues do not move suburban Latinx voters in odd-year and midterm cycles.
5. Ask the support question with a 1–5 scale. Mark it.
6. Confirm the polling place by name (not address) — Latinx voters in newer Gwinnett developments often confuse the assigned precinct with the closest school.
7. Hand them a Spanish bilingual leave-behind (no English-only literature). Thank them by name.

## Things that hurt response rate

- Scripted English greetings translated word-for-word into Spanish ("¿Tiene un momento?" reads as a sales call).
- Calling the voter by their first initial + last name combo from the voter file — Spanish surnames with two parts read as the file being wrong.
- Knocking after 8pm in evangelical neighborhoods (a meaningful share of Gwinnett Latinx households) — Wednesday and Sunday evenings are church nights.
- Asking about ICE, immigration enforcement, or "papers" before the voter raises the topic. The voter knows you do not have answers and will close the door.

## Cadence that converts

- **Round 1, 60 days out:** ID round. One contact, one ask: support level + voting plan question.
- **Round 2, 21 days out:** persuasion + early-vote pitch to 4s and 5s. SMS follow-up 48 hours later with the early-vote location.
- **Round 3, GOTV weekend:** door knock to confirmed 4s and 5s with the location and time of their early-vote site or polling place. Skip 1s and 2s entirely.

## Conversion benchmarks (Sun Belt suburbs, 2022 and 2024)

- Bilingual door knock to Latinx mid-prop voter: **9–12 percentage point lift** over no contact.
- English-only door knock to same voter: **2–3 point lift**.
- SMS follow-up after a bilingual door knock: **additional 1–2 point lift** if sent within 48 hours.
- SMS-only with no door precedes: **0.3–0.6 point lift** (basically background noise).

## When to pull the door and switch to phones

If the universe is geographically dispersed (more than 25% of doors per shift outside a 1.5-mile radius), or if literacy concerns make written leave-behinds unreliable, switch to bilingual phone banking with native-Spanish callers. The cost-per-conversion crosses over at roughly that dispersion threshold.
