# Spanish-Language Political Messaging Norms

**Source:** Powerbuilder curated corpus, distilled from Equis Research, Latino Decisions, Voto Latino, and field-tested copy from 2024 GA-07, FL-09, and AZ-CD3.
**Date:** 2025-11-02
**Document type:** messaging guide
**Topics:** spanish, messaging, latinx, copywriting, translation

## Hard rules

- **Never run English copy through machine translation as your final draft.** It will read as foreign — wrong register, wrong rhythm, the wrong words for the same concept across regions. Always have a native speaker rewrite, not translate.
- **Match the country/regional Spanish to your audience.** Gwinnett's Latinx population is majority Mexican-origin with sizable Salvadoran, Honduran, and Cuban cohorts. Default to neutral Mexican Spanish, avoid Castilian forms ("vosotros," "coger" outside Mexico), and avoid Cuban-specific slang outside Miami-Dade and Hudson County NJ.
- **Do not Anglicize.** "Vote por correo" not "vote by mail." "Centro de votación" not "polling place." "Boleta" not "ballot." If a term is locally known by the English word (e.g. "el waiver" in some immigration contexts), keep the English word — but do not invent code-switching.

## Tú vs. usted

- **Door knocks, peer-to-peer SMS, digital social copy:** use **tú-form** for warmth and equity. The volunteer at the door is your neighbor, not your superior.
- **Mail to seniors, formal phone scripts, official campaign letters from the candidate:** use **usted** for respect.
- **Direct mail to mixed-age households:** use usted. The grandmother opens the mail.
- **Never mix tú and usted in the same piece.** Pick one and hold it.

## Headline and CTA conventions

- Headlines that work: imperative ("Vote temprano"), inclusive first-person plural ("Hagamos que cuente"), or named direct address ("María, su voto cuenta esta semana").
- CTAs that work: a verb + a place + a date. "Vote temprano en [Location] hasta el [Date]." Avoid abstract CTAs like "Make your voice heard" — they translate as empty.
- Avoid the word "campaña" in the opening — it reads as a sales pitch. Lead with the issue.

## Issue framing that converts

- Economy: kitchen-table, family-budget framing. "El costo del huevo, la luz, la renta." Not "inflation."
- Healthcare: cost of a doctor visit, prescription costs, distance to a clinic. Not "Medicare expansion."
- Education: the kids' school by name, ESOL programs, the bus route, after-school. Not "education policy."
- Jobs: hourly wages, weekend hours, getting paid on time. Not "labor force participation."
- Immigration: only when raised by the voter. When raised, lead with "una vía justa para la gente que ya está aquí trabajando" — never with enforcement framing.

## What loses

- Polling stats in copy. Spanish-dominant voters discount US polls heavily. Use **stories**, not numbers.
- Acronyms (ACA, SNAP, EITC) untranslated.
- Photos of suits at podiums. Use photos of the candidate at a tienda, a panadería, a school pickup line.
- Long form. Keep door scripts to 90 seconds, mail to 150 words, SMS to 140 characters.

## Validated SMS templates (real volunteer copy, lightly anonymized)

> Hola [NOMBRE], soy María del equipo de [CANDIDATE]. ¿Sabía que puede votar temprano en [LOCATION] hasta el [DATE]? Responda STOP para no recibir más mensajes.

> Buenos días [NOMBRE]. Soy Carlos, voluntario aquí en Gwinnett. Le confirmo: su centro de votación el día de la elección es [PLACE]. Cualquier pregunta, escríbame. STOP para parar.

## Door-knock opener template

> Buenas, soy [FIRST_NAME] con [CAMPAIGN]. ¿Prefiere que sigamos en español o en inglés? — pause — Estoy por aquí esta semana hablando con vecinos sobre [LOCAL_ISSUE]. ¿Le importa si le pregunto qué piensa?
