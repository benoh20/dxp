# Channel Choice — Door vs. Phone vs. Text vs. Mail

**Source:** Powerbuilder curated corpus, distilled from Analyst Institute experimental archives 2010-2024.
**Date:** 2025-10-10
**Document type:** field operations guide
**Topics:** canvassing, phones, sms, mail, channel selection, gotv

## The single most-cited finding in voter contact research

A high-quality door knock from a trained, demographically-matched volunteer produces **8-10 percentage points** of turnout lift on a low-to-mid-propensity voter. Nothing else in the channel mix comes close per contact. Everything else in this document is about when you cannot or should not knock.

## Decision tree

**Knock the door when:**

- The universe is geographically dense (≥ 25 doors/hour achievable)
- You have demographically-matched volunteers (language, age, neighborhood familiarity)
- You are within 8 weeks of the election
- The audience is a mid-prop voter or a new registrant — high-prop voters do not need persuasion contact

**Phone bank when:**

- The universe is geographically dispersed (rural, exurban, or scattered urban)
- The contact ask is logistical (location confirm, ride-to-polls)
- The audience skews older — phone pickup rates above age 55 are 4-6x higher than under 35
- You need scale fast and have caller capacity

**Send SMS when:**

- Following up within 48 hours of a door knock (lift compounds)
- Sending day-of GOTV reminders to confirmed supporters
- Reaching under-35 voters who do not pick up the phone

**Send mail when:**

- The audience is over 55 and a homeowner — best ROI by far in this cohort
- You need to deliver complex info (sample ballot, location with map)
- The ask is from the candidate personally (a signed letter from the candidate to a senior is a high-trust touch)

**Do not bother with:**

- Cold SMS to under-35 voters with no prior touch (high opt-out, low conversion)
- Mail to under-30 renters (open rates below 20%)
- Phone calls to under-30 voters who have not opted in
- Door knocks to high-propensity already-confirmed supporters (you are wasting a slot)

## Cost-per-conversion benchmarks (2024 Sun Belt averages)

| Channel       | Cost / contact | Conversions / 100 contacts | Cost / conversion |
|---------------|---------------:|---------------------------:|------------------:|
| Door (paid)   | $7.50          | 9.2                        | $81               |
| Door (vol.)   | $1.20          | 9.2                        | $13               |
| Phone (paid)  | $1.40          | 2.8                        | $50               |
| Phone (vol.)  | $0.30          | 2.8                        | $11               |
| SMS p2p       | $0.08          | 0.6                        | $13               |
| Mail (1pc)    | $0.85          | 0.4–1.1 (varies w/ age)    | $77–$210          |
| Digital ad    | $0.012/imp.    | 0.04 (estimated)           | $30 (modeled)     |

A volunteer-driven canvass program is **always** the highest-leverage spend if the universe is dense enough to support it. Most campaigns under-invest in volunteer recruitment relative to paid channels.

## Compounding rules

- Door knock + SMS within 48 hours: +1-2 additional points over door alone
- Door + mail to seniors: +0.5-1 point
- Phone + SMS day-of: +1 point on supporters already at 4-5
- Mail alone to under-50: do not bother

## Worst common mistake

Spending two-thirds of the budget on digital ads to under-30 voters because "that's where they are." Digital is a low-conversion supplement, not a primary channel. The under-30 turnout gap closes through one-to-one volunteer contact, not through Facebook spend.
