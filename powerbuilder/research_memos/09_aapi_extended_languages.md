# AAPI Extended Languages: Japanese, Thai, Khmer, Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, Gujarati

**Source:** Powerbuilder curated corpus, distilled from APIAVote in-language field reports, AAPI Data subgroup civic surveys, Pew Research AAPI subgroup studies, SAALT (South Asian Americans Leading Together) policy briefs, Cambodian American civic engagement studies (e.g., Cambodian Mutual Assistance Association reports), and Analyst Institute in-language messaging summaries.
**Date:** 2026-04-28
**Document type:** field playbook + translation guidance, supplement to file 08
**Topics:** aapi, multi-language, japanese, thai, khmer, cambodian, urdu, bengali, bangla, punjabi, gujarati, south-asian, southeast-asian, civic engagement

## Why this file is separate from file 08

File 08 covers the five highest-volume AAPI languages: Chinese (Simplified and Traditional), Vietnamese, Korean, Tagalog, and Hindi. File 09 covers the **next tier of high-leverage languages** that meet at least one of these conditions: a community of more than 250,000 US adults, a population concentrated enough to swing a state legislative or municipal race, or a registration gap large enough that in-language outreach is the difference between a measurable contact and zero contact.

These languages are smaller in absolute population, but the **per-contact lift from in-language outreach is typically higher** than for Chinese or Hindi. The audience is less saturated by English-language civic messaging, has fewer bilingual adult-child intermediaries, and rewards a campaign that bothered to translate at all.

A single rule applies across every language in this file: **do not translate from one language in this set into another.** Khmer is not a dialect of Thai. Punjabi is not a dialect of Urdu or Hindi, even where the spoken vocabulary overlaps, because the script and the religious and political associations differ. Bengali script is not interchangeable with Hindi script. Each language gets its own native writer.

## How to decide which of these languages your program needs

Run this triage in order:

1. **Voter file or census tract analysis.** Pull tracts with >5 percent language-isolation in any of these languages (American Community Survey table B16001). If your target geography has none, drop the language.
2. **Partner check.** Ask the closest AAPI civic partner (APIAVote affiliate, SAALT chapter, local Cambodian or Lao mutual assistance association, gurdwara civic committee) whether they have voter contact lists or trusted messengers in the language. If not, in-language is unlikely to be deliverable on schedule even if the population justifies it.
3. **Cycle math.** If the persuasion universe in that language is below 1,500 voters, in-language is a relational organizing investment, not a paid-media or mail investment. Plan accordingly: the spend goes into a community organizer's salary, not into a translation vendor.
4. **Registration vs. turnout posture.** Khmer, Bengali, and Punjabi communities often have larger registration gaps than turnout gaps among the registered. Lead with registration drives and naturalization-cohort outreach (see file 03), not GOTV mail.

If a language clears all four checks, treat it as a first-class part of the program and apply the file-08 translation methodology (translate from strategy, not from English copy; back-translate; community readers, not commercial vendors).

## Japanese

**Population context:** Roughly 1.5 million Japanese Americans, with the largest concentrations in HI, CA, WA, NY, and IL. The community is unusual within AAPI in that it is **majority US-born and multi-generational** (Nisei, Sansei, Yonsei, Gosei). Foreign-born Japanese Americans (Issei and Shin-Issei) skew older and concentrate in the Bay Area, Greater Los Angeles, Honolulu, and Seattle.

**Language posture:** English is the dominant civic-engagement language for the community overall. In-language Japanese outreach is **targeted, not general**. It matters most for:
- Issei and Shin-Issei households over 65, especially first-generation post-1980 professional immigrants and their spouses.
- Voter registration drives at Japanese Christian churches, Buddhist temples (Jodo Shinshu and Soto Zen networks), and Japanese American Citizens League (JACL) chapters.
- Naturalization-cohort outreach in cities with Japanese consulate-adjacent expatriate communities.

**Tone:** formal register (敬語, keigo) for any voter over 60. Informal register reads as disrespectful and shuts down the conversation. Younger Japanese-American voters (Sansei and beyond) read in-language outreach as a heritage gesture rather than a primary channel; lead in English for them.

**Frame that works:** civic continuity and community legacy. Japanese-American civic identity is shaped by the WWII incarceration history and the redress movement; references to "our community's voice" and "passing forward what was earned" resonate. Avoid framing the vote as a transactional exchange (vote for X to get Y); the community reads that as low-trust politics.

**Sample door opener (Japanese, formal):** "こんにちは。私は[名前]と申します。今日は地域の選挙について短くお話を伺ってもよろしいでしょうか。" ("Hello. My name is [name]. May I briefly speak with you today about the upcoming community election?")

**Channel notes:** Rafu Shimpo (Los Angeles), North American Post (Seattle), and Hawai‘i Hochi remain meaningful for the over-65 audience. JACL chapters and Buddhist temple newsletters are higher-trust than commercial channels. Digital reach is limited; do not over-invest in Japanese-language paid social.

## Thai

**Population context:** Roughly 320,000 Thai Americans, concentrated in CA (especially Greater Los Angeles "Thai Town"), NV, TX, IL, FL, and the DC metro. About 65 percent foreign-born among adults. The community is younger and more recent than the Japanese community and has a much larger language-preference gap.

**Language posture:** Thai is the dominant household language for foreign-born adults and a meaningful preference language for the 50-and-over segment overall. **In-language Thai outreach is high-leverage and underused**: most national programs do not translate to Thai at all, so the lift from being the campaign that did is significant.

**Tone:** polite register with the **ครับ/ค่ะ (krap/ka)** sentence-final particles in any spoken or audio script (krap for a male speaker, ka for a female speaker). Omitting them reads as terse and impersonal. Use the **คุณ (khun)** honorific before names.

**Frame that works:** family well-being and community standing (หน้าตาของชุมชน, "the face of the community"). Thai civic culture places weight on community reputation and dignity; framing the vote as both a personal decision and a community act resonates. Avoid framing that emphasizes individual rights in isolation; pair rights language with community responsibility language.

**Sample door opener (Thai):** "สวัสดีค่ะ ดิฉันชื่อ [ชื่อ] วันนี้ขอเรียนถามสั้น ๆ เกี่ยวกับการเลือกตั้งในชุมชนของเราได้ไหมคะ" ("Hello, my name is [name]. May I briefly ask you today about the election in our community?")

**Channel notes:** Thai Buddhist temples (วัด, wat) are the highest-trust civic infrastructure; voter registration tables at Sunday merit-making services reach more eligible voters per hour than door knocks in Thai-dense tracts. Thai-language Facebook groups (especially Greater LA and DC metro community groups) are active and reach the 30-to-55 segment. Thai TV USA and Sereechai Newspaper reach the over-50 segment.

## Khmer (Cambodian)

**Population context:** Roughly 350,000 Cambodian Americans, concentrated in Long Beach (CA), Lowell (MA), Stockton/Modesto (CA), Philadelphia/Bristol (PA), Providence (RI), and the Twin Cities. The community is **trauma-shaped**: most adult immigrants are post-1979 refugees and their children, and civic outreach must be built on that history rather than imported from a generic AAPI playbook.

**Language posture:** Khmer is the dominant household language for foreign-born adults and a meaningful preference language for US-born adults raised by Khmer-dominant parents. The registration gap is large (estimated 20 to 30 points behind the AAPI average in several Cambodian-dense cities, per local civic partners). **In-language outreach is the entry condition, not an enhancement.**

**Tone:** respectful register with age-appropriate honorifics (**លោក/លោកស្រី**, lok/lok srey for adults; **ពូ/មីង**, pou/ming for older neighbors). Younger US-born Cambodian-American voters often code-switch and accept English; older voters often do not.

**Frame that works:** **community survival and the next generation**. The Cambodian-American civic identity is shaped by the Khmer Rouge genocide and the refugee resettlement story. Framing the vote as protecting the community's children and grandchildren resonates deeply. Avoid framing that involves any reference to "the regime," "the party," or "the government" without context; those words carry weight that English-language messaging does not anticipate.

**Sample door opener (Khmer):** "ជំរាបសួរលោក/លោកស្រី។ ខ្ញុំឈ្មោះ [ឈ្មោះ]។ តើខ្ញុំអាចសួរលោកបន្តិចអំពីការបោះឆ្នោតសហគមន៍យើងបានទេ?" ("Hello, sir/ma'am. My name is [name]. May I briefly ask you about our community's upcoming election?")

**Channel notes:** Cambodian Mutual Assistance Associations (CMAAs) are the central civic infrastructure in every major Cambodian-American city; partner with the local CMAA before any door program. Khmer-language Buddhist temples reach the over-50 segment for registration drives. Khmer-language radio (KKLH, KKHL in greater LA; community shows in Lowell and Philadelphia) reaches the over-45 audience. Khmer-language Facebook is active in the 25-to-50 segment but fragmented across many small groups; relational organizing through community leaders is more efficient than paid social.

## Urdu

**Population context:** Roughly 600,000 Urdu speakers in the US, primarily Pakistani-American (and a meaningful share of Indian Muslim families and Bangladeshi households for whom Urdu is a literary or religious second language). Concentrated in NY/NJ metro, Houston, DFW, Chicago, Greater DC, Atlanta, and the Bay Area.

**Language posture:** Spoken Urdu is mutually intelligible with spoken Hindi at the conversational level, but **the script and the religious and political associations are different**, and using the wrong one is a trust-breaking error. Use Urdu (Nastaliq script, right-to-left) for Pakistani-American and Indian Muslim households. Do not substitute Hindi (Devanagari) script and assume the audience can read it; many cannot, and those who can will read the substitution as the campaign not knowing who they are.

**Tone:** respectful register using **آپ (aap)** rather than the informal **تم (tum)**. Use **صاحب (sahib)** or **صاحبہ (sahiba)** as a default honorific for adults whose names you do not know. Religious framing is often welcome but should be careful: avoid invoking specific theological positions; "your voice for your community" works across observance levels.

**Frame that works:** family, community standing, and the next generation's place in America. Pakistani-American civic identity in the post-2001 period has been shaped by surveillance and immigration anxiety; framing the vote as a way to be visible and counted on the community's own terms resonates. Avoid framing that asks the voter to choose between identities (American vs. Pakistani, religious vs. civic). Pair, do not contrast.

**Sample mailer headline (Urdu, Nastaliq):** "آپ کی آواز، آپ کی برادری کے لیے" ("Your voice, for your community.")

**Channel notes:** mosques (especially Sunni Hanafi and Shia Ithna Ashari communities) are high-trust civic spaces, but partner relationships matter more than walk-in canvassing. Urdu-language WhatsApp groups (family chats, mosque parent groups, business association groups) carry political information faster than any paid channel. Urdu-language print (Pakistan Times USA, Urdu Times) reaches the over-50 audience. South Asian community radio in NJ, Houston, and the Bay Area is meaningful and overlaps with the Hindi-language audience but should be bought separately because the listenership preferences and trust patterns differ.

## Bengali (Bangla)

**Population context:** Roughly 450,000 Bengali speakers in the US, primarily Bangladeshi-American with a meaningful Indian-Bengali (West Bengal-origin) population. Concentrated in NYC (especially Queens, Bronx, Brooklyn), Paterson NJ, Hamtramck MI, Detroit metro, DFW, and the DC metro. Bangladeshi-American is one of the **fastest-growing AAPI subgroups by registration**, particularly in NYC outer boroughs.

**Language posture:** Bengali (Bangla) is written in Bengali script and is **not interchangeable with Hindi or Urdu**. Spoken vocabulary overlaps with Hindi for some everyday terms but the literary register and civic vocabulary are distinct. Use Bengali script for any household where the adults are Bangladeshi-born or where the file flags Bangla as the household language. Do not default to English for Bangladeshi households over 50; the language gap is real.

**Tone:** respectful register using **আপনি (apni)** rather than the informal **তুমি (tumi)**. Use **দাদা (dada)** for an older man you address as a brother-figure, **দিদি (didi)** for an older woman as sister-figure, when the relationship warrants it; use plain **মহাশয় (mahashay)** or just no address term for a stranger.

**Frame that works:** civic dignity and the immigrant family's stake in the city or state. Bangladeshi-American voters in NYC, Paterson, and Hamtramck have built civic visibility recently through municipal organizing (Bangladeshi-American council members in NYC, mayoral race in Hamtramck, school board elections in Paterson). Frame the vote as continuing that visibility rather than as a generic civic duty.

**Sample door opener (Bengali):** "নমস্কার, আমার নাম [নাম]। আমি কি আপনার সাথে আমাদের সম্প্রদায়ের আসন্ন নির্বাচন নিয়ে অল্প সময়ের জন্য কথা বলতে পারি?" ("Hello, my name is [name]. May I speak with you briefly about our community's upcoming election?")

**Channel notes:** Bangladeshi mosques and cultural associations (especially in Queens and Paterson) are central civic infrastructure. Bangla-language WhatsApp and Facebook groups are dense and active in the 25-to-55 segment. Bangla-language community newspapers (Weekly Bangalee, Thikana, Bangla Patrika) reach the over-45 audience and are read across the household, including by adult children translating for parents. Bangla-language local TV (TBN24 in NYC) reaches the over-50 segment.

## Punjabi

**Population context:** Roughly 350,000 to 500,000 Punjabi speakers in the US (estimates vary because the census undercounts), primarily Sikh Punjabi-Americans with smaller Punjabi Hindu and Punjabi Muslim populations. Concentrated in CA Central Valley (Yuba City, Fresno, Bakersfield, Stockton, Sacramento), the Bay Area, NY metro (especially Queens and Long Island), and the Seattle and DFW metros. Punjabi-American is one of the most **politically engaged South Asian subgroups per capita**.

**Language posture:** Punjabi in the US is overwhelmingly **Gurmukhi script** (the script associated with Sikh religious texts) for Sikh Punjabi-Americans, and **Shahmukhi script** (Perso-Arabic, similar to Urdu) for Punjabi Muslim households of Pakistani origin. Default to Gurmukhi for Sikh households and Central Valley Punjabi-American audiences; default to Shahmukhi only when you have a confirmed Pakistani-Punjabi household.

**Tone:** respectful register using **ਤੁਸੀਂ (tusi)** rather than the informal **ਤੂੰ (tu)**. Use **ਜੀ (ji)** as a polite suffix after names and titles; omitting it reads as flat. **Sat Sri Akal (ਸਤ ਸ੍ਰੀ ਅਕਾਲ)** is the standard greeting for Sikh households and signals respect when used by a non-Sikh canvasser. **Waheguru ji ka khalsa, waheguru ji ki fateh** is a religious greeting; do not use it as a canvasser unless you are part of the community, because it reads as performative.

**Frame that works:** **seva** (selfless service) and community standing (**izzat**). Sikh civic identity is shaped by the principles of seva and community accountability; framing the vote as service to the community works. Punjabi-American civic identity is also shaped by the post-2001 surveillance period and by ongoing immigration policy anxiety; the same notes about visibility from the Urdu and Bengali sections apply here.

**Sample door opener (Punjabi, Gurmukhi):** "ਸਤ ਸ੍ਰੀ ਅਕਾਲ ਜੀ। ਮੇਰਾ ਨਾਮ [ਨਾਮ] ਹੈ। ਕੀ ਮੈਂ ਤੁਹਾਡੇ ਨਾਲ ਆਉਣ ਵਾਲੀਆਂ ਚੋਣਾਂ ਬਾਰੇ ਥੋੜੀ ਗੱਲ ਕਰ ਸਕਦਾ/ਸਕਦੀ ਹਾਂ?" ("Sat Sri Akal, ji. My name is [name]. May I speak with you briefly about the upcoming elections?")

**Channel notes:** gurdwaras are the central civic infrastructure for Sikh Punjabi-American communities; voter registration tables at Sunday langar reach more eligible voters per hour than any other channel. Punjabi-language radio (Radio Punjab in the Central Valley and Bay Area, Sher-e-Punjab in Seattle, Punjabi Radio USA) reaches the over-45 audience. Punjabi-language YouTube channels and WhatsApp groups reach the 25-to-55 segment and are higher-trust than paid social. Print remains meaningful for the over-55 audience (Punjab Times USA, Sher-e-Punjab newspaper).

## Gujarati

**Population context:** Roughly 400,000 Gujarati speakers in the US, primarily Indian-American Gujarati Hindus and Gujarati Jains, with a smaller Gujarati Muslim population (often Bohra and Khoja communities). Concentrated in NJ (Edison, Jersey City), NYC metro, Greater Philadelphia, DFW, Houston, Atlanta, Chicago, and the Bay Area. Gujarati-American business community presence (motels, pharmacies, small business) is unusually high and shapes civic networks.

**Language posture:** Gujarati script. The 25-to-50 segment is largely English-dominant for civic content but reads in-language outreach as a respect signal. The over-50 segment, especially women in joint-family households, is more often Gujarati-dominant and benefits from in-language mail and door scripts.

**Tone:** respectful register using **તમે (tame)** rather than the informal **તું (tu)**. **ભાઈ (bhai)** and **બહેન (behn)** as suffixes for adult community members are warmer than English equivalents; use them for community-rooted door scripts but not for cold paid mail.

**Frame that works:** family business, community contribution, and the next generation. Gujarati-American civic identity is shaped by entrepreneurship and community philanthropy networks (Patel motel-owner networks, BAPS Swaminarayan and Pushtimarg temple communities, Jain center networks). Frame the vote as protecting what the family has built and contributing to the broader community in the same register as charitable giving.

**Sample mailer headline (Gujarati):** "તમારો અવાજ, તમારા સમુદાય માટે" ("Your voice, for your community.")

**Channel notes:** temple newsletters and community association directories (Gujarati Samaj chapters) are higher-trust than commercial channels. Gujarati-language WhatsApp groups are dense, especially family groups and business association groups in NJ and Houston. Gujarati-language print (Gujarat Times, Gujarati Dharti) reaches the over-50 audience. Gujarati-language paid social is sparse and mostly serves regional Bollywood-adjacent content; civic content underperforms there compared to relational organizing.

## Cross-language operational notes (supplement to file 08)

- **Refugee-origin communities (Khmer, Hmong if added later, some Bangladeshi cohorts) require trauma-informed canvassing.** Train canvassers to expect and accept silence, to never push past a closed door, and to recognize when an in-language conversation is reaching a household elder who needs the adult child to mediate.
- **Religious infrastructure is the dominant civic infrastructure** for most communities in this file (Buddhist temples for Thai and Khmer; mosques for Urdu-speaking and many Bengali households; gurdwaras for Sikh Punjabi; Hindu and Jain temples for Gujarati; Buddhist temples and JACL chapters for Japanese). Partner relationships are slow to build and quick to lose; do not arrive in the last six weeks of a cycle expecting a registration table.
- **WhatsApp is the connective tissue** for Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, and Gujarati communities. Family group chats move political information faster than any paid channel. The campaign's job is to give a trusted community member shareable in-language assets, not to push paid messages into spaces where they read as spam.
- **Universe sizing is harder than for the file-08 languages** because voter files rarely flag these communities accurately. Combine surname-based modeling (with documented error rates), self-reported language preference where available, and partner-supplied lists. Document the modeling choices for c3 reporting.
- **Never bundle these languages in a single budget line.** If the program plans to translate to Khmer, Punjabi, and Gujarati, that is three separate line items, three separate vendor or community-reader relationships, and three separate review cycles. Bundling produces the auto-translation outcome the file-08 methodology specifically warns against.

## What does not work (additions specific to this file)

- Treating Hindi as a stand-in for Urdu, Bengali, Punjabi, or Gujarati. Each language has its own script and political associations.
- Translating one canvasser's door script into all six (or seven) languages from a single English source. The source-of-truth has to be the strategy brief, not the English script.
- Skipping religious-infrastructure partners and going straight to commercial in-language paid social. Trust is concentrated in temples, mosques, gurdwaras, and community associations; commercial digital reaches a much smaller, younger, and already-engaged audience.
- Asking a Hindi-speaking translator to handle Urdu (or vice versa) because the spoken languages overlap. The script and the cultural register diverge, and the audience reads the substitution.
- Buying generic "South Asian" or "Southeast Asian" media. Each community has its own newspapers, radio shows, and digital networks; combined buys are diluted and signal that the campaign does not know the audience.
