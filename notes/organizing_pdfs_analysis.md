### 1. Common Frameworks Across the Corpus (Concepts Repeating in 3+ Documents)

**Theory of Change** — PDFs 2, 3, 5, 6, 10  
The "If we do ___, then ___ will happen" sentence frame appears explicitly across five documents and is implied in all others. It functions as the universal planning scaffold: before any tactic, the organizer must articulate a causal logic connecting action to outcome. The 3 power types (Through/Over/With) from re:power give ToC a structural typology that the other documents lack.

**Ladder/Funnel of Engagement** — PDFs 7, 8, 9, 10  
The concept of a progression from low-commitment to high-commitment participation appears in four documents under different names (ladder, funnel, matrix, spectrum of allies). The strategic imperative is always the same: move people up. The re:power documents name and diagram it; the others embed the logic without naming it.

**Power Mapping** — PDFs 5, 6, 9, 10  
Two distinct power map models appear: Ruckus Society's 4-quadrant matrix (powerful/powerless × aligned/not aligned) and the Wellstone 10-point scale model (FWD.us). Both ask organizers to plot decision-makers and stakeholders in two dimensions — amount of power and positional alignment. This is the dominant analytical tool for target selection.

**Base vs. Persuadable Audience Segmentation** — PDFs 1, 2, 3, 4, 10  
Every campaign document distinguishes between the base (people already aligned, to be activated) and persuadables (people who could be moved, requiring different messaging). PDF 2 provides the most detailed persona ("Tough Cookies"). PDF 4 treats audience identification as the foundational act before any digital work begins.

**Coalition/Ally Strategy** — PDFs 1, 9, 10  
The spectrum of allies, partner tagging, and coalition coordination tactics appear across three documents. The logic: no campaign has enough power alone; identifying and moving allies is as important as identifying and pressuring targets.

**Data & Measurement** — PDFs 3, 9, 10  
Measurement appears in three documents: PDF 3 (QR codes, pre/post surveys, Salesforce tracking), PDF 9 (debrief step, measurable action demands), PDF 10 (data management checklist, campaign database). All three treat data as infrastructure, not afterthought.

### 3. Notable Gaps

**Covered Shallowly:**
- **Relational organizing / 1:1 conversations** — The core "have a conversation, identify self-interest, make an ask" methodology of classic community organizing (Alinsky, IAF tradition) is not named or taught directly in any document. The re:power materials gesture at it (audience understanding, constituency) but never teach the 1:1 as a distinct tool.
- **A/B testing and analytics** — Named in PDF 6's curriculum outline but not developed in the pages read. Digital optimization methodology is mentioned as a topic but not explained.
- **Rapid response** — Appears in PDF 6's curriculum and PDF 10's deportation defense checklist but no document teaches the decision-making process for rapid response.
- **Volunteer and leader development** — The Ladder of Engagement implies a pipeline from supporter to leader, but none of the documents teach how to develop or retain leaders once identified.

**Missing Entirely:**
- **Voter file / VAN / NGP** — No document mentions the Voter Activation Network, voter file access, or voter contact scoring. These are central to electoral campaigns but absent from this corpus.
- **Census data and demographic targeting** — No document explains how to use Census data or geographic demographic data for organizing decisions.
- **Relational data / contact database hygiene** — While PDF 10 mentions "data management," no document explains how to build, clean, or use a relational contact database at scale.
- **Canvassing / door-to-door scripts** — Physical field tactics are absent. No door knock scripts, turf-cutting methodology, or field program management.
- **Electoral vs. issue campaign distinctions** — The 3 powers framework gestures at this but no document explains the legal or strategic differences between c3, c4, and electoral campaign work.
- **Coalition governance** — PDF 1 shows a coalition in action but no document explains how to structure coalition decision-making, accountability, or shared governance.
- **Opposition research** — Named implicitly in "know the target" steps, but no document teaches how to research targets or prepare counter-messaging.
- **Fundraising mechanics** — PDF 10 mentions "grassroots fundraising" as a tactic but no document explains how to run an online fundraising campaign.

### 4. Pedagogical Patterns — How Do These Documents Teach?

**Popular education methodology is universal.** All re:power documents (PDFs 4–7) explicitly use popular education conventions: start from participants' existing knowledge, use personal stories as entry points, employ discussion prompts before delivering content, and frame new concepts against lived experience. This is not incidental — it's a political pedagogy that treats learners as knowledge-holders rather than empty vessels.

**Worked examples anchor abstract frameworks.** The Theory of Change formula ("If we do X…") is always paired with real historical examples (Florida rights restoration, bail fund, wage campaign). Abstract frameworks without examples are rare in this corpus.

**Visual artifacts carry meaning.** The power map, ladder diagram, and spectrum of allies are all visual tools designed to be drawn, filled in, or printed as worksheets. The pedagogy assumes a physical or digital whiteboard. Documents that lack visuals (PDFs 8–9) compensate with numbered checklists.

**Sequencing matters.** re:power's curriculum sequence is deliberate: values/power analysis comes before tool selection. PDF 5's explicit framing ("What is your goal? What is your path? Who are your people? And *then* you can pick tools.") expresses a pedagogical principle that runs through the entire corpus.

**Short, actionable over comprehensive.** The best-designed documents (PDFs 8, 9) are 2–3 pages and contain no unnecessary explanation. The longer workbook-style documents (PDFs 7, 10) use white space, worksheets, and visual breaks to chunk content. No document assumes more than 90 minutes of sustained attention per topic.

**Story as proof and method.** PDFs 1, 3, 7, and 10 all treat personal storytelling as both a campaign tactic (collect and amplify stories) and a teaching method (use real stories to illustrate concepts). The collection and use of stories is simultaneously a campaign output and a pedagogical tool.

### 5. Specific UX / Training Ideas for an AI Campaign Tool

**Idea 1: Strategy-First Intake (mirroring re:power's sequence)**  
Before an AI tool offers any templates or content help, it should ask: What type of power are you building? (Through / Over / With), Who is your target?, Who is your base?, and What is your theory of change? This mirrors the deliberate sequencing the re:power curriculum uses to prevent tool-before-strategy errors. The AI should refuse to generate social posts until these fields are answered.

**Idea 2: Ladder of Engagement as Campaign Architecture**  
Campaign plans could be structured using the ladder as a UI metaphor — each "rung" is a campaign moment with specific content and conversion goals. The AI could suggest appropriate content for each rung (low-commitment: petition; mid: event RSVP; high: volunteer sign-up) and flag when a campaign is missing rungs.

**Idea 3: PSAA Content Generator**  
Every content generation prompt should map to Problem → Solution → Action → Ask. The AI could parse a user's draft and tag each sentence by PSAA function, then flag missing components ("Your post has a problem and an action, but no ask.").

**Idea 4: Power Map as Onboarding Worksheet**  
The power map (in either the 4-quadrant or Wellstone model) could be an interactive onboarding step for new campaigns. The AI could walk the user through plotting their stakeholders and then use that data to personalize subsequent recommendations (who to target, who to cultivate, who to neutralize).

**Idea 5: Canonical Vocabulary as Glossary + Writing Style**
